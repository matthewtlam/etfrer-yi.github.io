Introduction

Originally, our timeline was to be divided into events of three different categories:

  1. Company scandals/lawsuits
  2. Laws/regulation
  3. Technologies

For the company scandals, we were aiming for scandals that were significant or milestones in their own rights. To do so, we decided to favor big companies that had to pay a lot of fines (tens and hundreds of millions) and decided to loook for class-action lawsuits where a lot of users were concered.

Also, we also tried to find older scandals of related to privacy and surveillance that were significant; finding such older scandals, we thought, would allow us to track how the nature of privacy-related scandals evolved over time.

Finally, it is important to note that although there are many data breaches that could have been included, we decided to pay less attention to them. The reason is simple: it could be the case that a company's data got leaked due to a hacking/cybersecurity attack, but it might not have been the company's fault. A company could be reasonably moral/lawful in their handling of data privacy yet suffer from an unfortunate high-level attack that compromised their security, for example, so it is more difficult to establish moral bankruptcy.

Ultimately, we also found scandals that do not involve big tech, to have greater variety in our scandals.

As for the laws/regulation, they were mainly U.S.-based. This was mainly driven by the fact that a lot of the companies we found were big tech companies based in the U.S.. Exceptions exist, however, such as GDPR, which has wide influence in Europe.

When we tried to think of the underlying technologies behind the scandals, we realized that they sorted themselves into three different categories:

  1. General
  2. Biometrics
  3. Location tracking

While "Biometrics" and "Location tracking" technologies make sense, the "General" category requires explanation. Think, for example, of a company that collects data from its users unlawfully and stores the data. That company might store the phone number that the user enters for 2FA authentication. In that case, what are the technologies involved? An API that makes a POST request? The programming language used? The SQL database? None of these are very interesting, so the "General" category is meant to broadly encompass events of all kinds that are not related to "Biometrics" and "Location tracking".

It is also interesting that our project goes beyond what the course material covers, yet it is deeply linked to class concepts. Location tracking, for example, was the subject of an earlier reading where a Catholic priest was determined to have used Grindr. Biometrics, of course, have been covered in the form of facial recognition mentioned in a variety of articles like the one on Uighurs in China or the early MIT article on Johannesburg's surveillance cameras.

Ultimately, our final timeline encompasses 9 categories that are a combination of the the three original categories and the three later categories:

  1. General - Scandals
  2. Biometrics - Scandals
  3. Location tracking - Scandals

  4. General - Technologies
  5. Biometrics - Technologies
  6. Location tracking - Technologies

  7. General - Location tracking
  8. Biometrics - Location tracking
  9. Location tracking - Location tracking

Conclusion

There are a variety of conclusions that we can draw from our timeline:

  1. A lot of scandals involving big tech and big fine are very recent, ending around 2021-2022. This is illustrated by the flurry of scandals post-2020. The emergence of major class-action lawsuits and their settlements could reflect a general trend of big tech being very loose with regards to data privacy or a trend towards tighter reins on privacy as revealed by privacy laws capable of fining such companies.
  2. A surprising number of scandals are related to unlawful biometric data collection, mostly driven by Illinois' BIPA law (2008 on the timeline). Google, Snap, Tiktok, and Facebook/Meta have all been involved in biometrics related scandals (see "(BIOMETRIC) SCANDALS" section).
  3. Cookies, originally designed with good intentions (see "(GENERAL) TECHNOLOGY" section, ~1994-1996), now are frowned upon and are gradually being deprecated and replaced (see "(GENERAL) TECHNOLOGY" section, ~2015-2022). Third-party cookies are the main culprit, with two scandals involving third-party cookies in the "(GENERAL) SCANDALS" section (Google (Youtube) and COPPA in 2019; Facebook & cookies in 2012).
  4. Facebook/Meta has earned their bad reputation with respect to data/privacy management, with 4 scandals/lawsuits (Cambridge Analytica in 2014; Facebook & cookies in 2012; Facebook & biometrics in 2015; Facebook and location tracking in 2018).
  5. Location tracking, curiously, were found to be the topic of two lawsuits with big tech. See Google & location tracking and Facebook & location tracking, both in 2018 on the "(LOCATION TRACKING) SCANDALS" row. (Please ZOOM in to distinguish these two).
  6. Laws tend to be outpaced by the technology they are meant to help regulate. This is best illustrated by Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986, which was meant to help regulate government monitoring of data, but due to specific technologies being listed (wiretaps) it did nothing to prevent emails being targeted
  7. Laws protecting individual rights are repealed after national security crises like 9/11. This is best demonstrated through the patriot Act which destroyed precedent set by cases like Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents, Keith Case and laws such as Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA)
